SCG $5K Kaldheim Championship Qualifier: Exploratory Data Analysis

Joseph Crouson

Dataset

A comprehensive data set of deck rankings and each respective card list.
This data is provided by MTGMelee and the SCG $5K Kaldheim Tournament. It contains all the tournament results for each player, the deck they played and the cards they played in the deck. This tournament contained 272 players, with 33 distinct deck and 351 distinct cards. I have been a long fan of Magic the Gathering, so these visualizations are a gift to the community of players that want to better understand the current metagame and what decks/cards are performing well this season.

The data is available online at: https://mtgmelee.com/Tournament/View/5254

Initial Analysis Questions

  1. Do any Decks noticeably outperform others?
  2. Do any Cards noticeably outperform others?
  3. Is there a correlation between Win % and Card Selection?

Discoveries & Insights

These first two visualizations are a simple Win Loss and Ratio comparison between the decks. This visualization shows what decks had the best rates in the tournament as a whole, meaning we are not concidering the number of players playing the deck or how far in the tournament they got (though details on demand gives us that information). We see here what archetypes stand out as net winners in the tournament.

The next visualization shows us the average rank a deck archetype obtained, labled by the number of players playing the specific deck archetype. We can see the gradual slope as the average rank increases, but the number of players playing the archetypes is sporadic, telling us no clear deck that stands above all others, all though some are more popular than others.

This visualization dispalys deck archetype popularity and its correponding win percentage. We see that the most popular decks are the ones with a positive win percentage, but also decks with a lower count (meaning decks that are new brews, solo brews, or of a niche archetype) are spread around the win percentage axis. This reinforces the fact that there is a good variaty of archetypes capable of being viable in the current meta. This plot will be used multiple times in the future to see how deck popularity and win percentage are coupled with card selection.

It is with this visualization that we break into a story of cards. Here we can visually compare a cards popularity among others in this tournament...and we see one such card stand tall above its peers. Bonecrusher Giant has been a subject of scrutiny for a while now, with critiques that "it does to much" and "has to much power for a single card". This visualization shows us that people believe those critiques. But is it true?

This visualization seems like a mess but with Tableaus interactive filtering it dispalys alot of information. This plot is used later to show card popularity by deck archetype. We see that there are a collection of cards that have both a large play rate and large card count. This visualization is more usefull in a dashboard view with multiple visualizations because it can better show the hard upper bound to a cards playability (a deck can only have 4 of one card). This allows us to see that when a card is played, how IMPORTANT the player base believes the card is to the decks success. Again though...Bonecrusher Giant stands above all others.

In this visualization we can view a cards play percentage in each deck. This has the effect of telling us what cards an archetype relies on and use most frequently. Used later in a dashboard view.

Here we plot a cards win percentage against its popularity, just as we did before with the decks. This is another visualization that will be used in a dashboard view down the line. But because a card can appear in multiple decks it shows a better representation of a cards influence in this meta. You can see a similar shape to the deck win percentage vs popularity, with a right shift off center. This is due to the fact that a player, even if they drop out in round 4 or the semi finals, will have a positive win rate. That is not to say that there should be a more true center, what it means is there is a valid bias in the direction of a card seeing more wins if it's playing well. What is important is to see how the cards appear relative to one another. Can you guess who is at the top of this plot!?

Before we took a card and saw what decks played it by percentage. In this visualization we take a deck archetype and break it down to the cards in it by percentage. From this visualization we can see what cards in a deck archetype are fundamental to what makes the archetype what it is.

This dashboard is a collection of visualizations, this has the power to answer harder and more interesting questions than single visualizations ever could. In this particular image, the linked filters can give us a better understaning of what a cards impact is in these decks. We select Bonecrusher Giant and select the top 4 decks that play it the most, and we see they have "very good" win rates. We can also see other cards that are contributing the most with Bonecrusher Giant to the decks win percentage.

In this dashboard view we select multiple decks and see how their cards stack up in popularity and win percentage. We see winning decks just play the stong cards, with the most popular one being Bonecrusher Giant.

In the same dashboard we can explore the answer to the question on everyones mind, how does Bonecrusher Giant effect the decks it's played in. Selecting only Bonecrusher Giant from the card pie reveals that the decks that play it are very...very favored to win. The plot on the right only tells us information for the single card, and it's information we already know. Bonecrusher Giant is very popular, and a very strong card this season.

Summary

The visualizations of this tournament are a first look at how results data can be visualized and used to guage the MTG metagame. Following the steps of EDA we discovered the power of Bonecrusher Giant, visually encoded popularity against win percetage for decks and their cards, finally we got to see how a cards presense in a deck affected the outcome (though there still might be unknown variables). Correlation does not equal causation is a common saying among data scientists, I think Bonecrusher Giant wants to have a word with those data scientists!

Further work would be to explore and visualize deck matchups, see how land count effects win rate, and what effect being on the play/draw has on the games outcome (if that data is available anywhere).
